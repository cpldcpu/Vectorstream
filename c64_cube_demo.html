Pipeline

Both approaches consume the same per-frame ideal rasterization (the leftmost panel), which fills three visible cube faces into a 160×200 multicolor framebuffer of values {0,1,2,3} = {bg, MC1, MC2, color-RAM}. Opposite cube faces share a color so any rotation shows ≤ 3 face colors. The framebuffer is then chunked into 4×8 char tiles. Approach 1 snaps each tile to the nearest entry in its color-pair bucket within the 256-char atlas. Approach 2 maintains a persistent slot pool with LRU eviction — a tile whose pattern is already in the pool just reuses its slot, only misses count as regenerations.

Reading the stats

pixel RMS = √(Σ(diff²)/N) over all 32,000 mc cells vs the ideal. pattern mismatch = mean Hamming distance of the placed char vs the ideal tile (out of 32 cells). regens this frame is the live pool-miss count for Approach 2; the smoothed average feeds est. cycles (140 cycles per generated edge char). working set is how many slots are touched this frame — the "naive regen-everything" cost. The chart below quantifies the gap as a function of rotation rate: how much coherence saves once angle changes are slow enough that most patterns survive across frames. RESET POOL starts over cold; the first frame is always expensive. RUN SWEEP runs a headless benchmark across 13 rotation rates from 1°/s to 500°/s and plots the curve.

Single-axis rotation and atlas optimization

Constraining rotation to one axis sharply changes the distribution of edge orientations the rasterizer produces, so the atlas can in principle be retuned. Empirical histogram for the cube under Y-only rotation (60 samples): ~30% of edges land at exactly 90° (line direction) — the 4 Y-aligned edges of the cube which stay vertical regardless of yaw — and ~21% land within ±5° of horizontal (the 8 X/Z edges projected through perspective). ≥51% of edge tiles concentrate at exact cardinals. Under X-only rotation, the distribution flips (33% near 0°, 24% near 90°). Free rotation spreads broadly with no dominant peak.

The polarity-pairing trap. A first attempt at an axial atlas (angles {−15°, 0°, 15°, 75°, 90°, 105°}) underperformed the uniform 6×7 by ~2× on Y-only rotation. The fix: every line orientation must appear with both gradient polarities. The line generator's "angle" parameter is the gradient direction, so θ and θ+180° produce the same line orientation but with the valA/valB sides swapped. The pair-bucket matcher uses one fixed ordering per color pair (e.g. {bg, MC1}), so a tile showing "MC1-above-bg" needs gradient 90° while "bg-above-MC1" needs gradient 270°. The uniform 6×7's angles {0°, 60°, 120°, 180°, 240°, 300°} happen to pair up as 3 orientations × 2 polarities — that's a structural reason it works, not just broad coverage. The axial layouts encode this explicitly.

The redesigned axial-h8v6 wins across the board for the cube. Two bugs were fixed: (1) the line generator's Math.sin(Math.PI) returns 1.22e-16 rather than 0, introducing a faint pseudo-tilt at "vertical" gradient angles that the matcher then preferred over the true verticals for some sub-pixel positions — the source of the comb-on-vertical-edge artifact. Cardinal sin/cos values are now snapped to exact zero. (2) The 4-line layout had no near-vertical tilts (only ±15° from horizontal), leaving cube edges slightly tilted from vertical under free rotation with nowhere to match cleanly. The 6-orientation rebuild adds ±15° tilts from both axes and replaces the partially-degenerate vertical offsets {−1.5, ±0.5, 1.5} with column-boundary-snapped {−1, 0, 1}. Net: 0 degenerate slots (was 12) and every entry contributes information.

Pushing further: the corner-tile insight. Asking whether Y-only-only specialization gives more room turned up something broader. Under Y-only rotation on the cube, ~47% of edge tiles are exactly vertical and ~10% near-horizontal — but ~25% of tiles measured by line-fitting land at 21°, 26°, 35°, 45°, etc. Those aren't real edges. They're corner tiles at cube vertices, where two cardinal edges meet in an L-shape. PCA-fitting a line through an L gives 45°. The matcher, doing Hamming-distance search, is also picking 45° patterns as the least-bad straight-line approximation for these L-shapes. So residual error is dominated by corner-tile mismatch, not edge-orientation mismatch. Dedicated 45° diagonal entries help.

The corner-diag layout drops the ±15° tilts of axial-h8v6 entirely and spends the freed budget on dense ±45° diagonal coverage (6+6 and 5+5 offsets per polarity). Result: it beats axial-h8v6 on essentially every shape × rotation-mode combination by 10-30%. Y-only cube goes from 0.20% → 0.17% (-15%); Y-only octahedron from 0.86% → 0.71% (-18%); Y-only icosahedron from 1.11% → 0.99% (-11%). The narrowly-targeted Y-only design turned out to be a generally better atlas — the ±15° near-cardinal tilts in axial-h8v6 are actually too close to cardinal to help with the L-corner case that dominates real residual error. The 30° tilts implicit in the uniform 6×7 (gradient angles 60°/120° produce line orientations 150° = -30° from horizontal) sit between the two and do middling on both axes.

The floor for any straight-line atlas seems to be around 0.17% pixel mismatch on the cube — corner tiles inherently have non-line structure (an L is two line segments meeting at a vertex), and no single straight-line atlas entry can represent them exactly. Pushing below that floor would require a different atlas structure entirely — explicit corner-pattern entries, or a hybrid where the matcher knows whether a tile is "edge-like" or "vertex-like" and selects from different atlas regions accordingly.
